Platform & Architecture

Decoupled Verification Architecture

CEYO operates beside inference systems to generate deterministic, integrity-sealed artifacts representing defined inference-time context under explicit policy scope. It does not modify model weights or intervene in decision logic.

[01] Attachment at the Inference Boundary

Sidecar / Gateway / Wrapper

CEYO integrates at the inference boundary through a sidecar, gateway, or API wrapper configuration. This posture is intentional: evidence infrastructure should not require mutation of model weights, training pipelines, or core inference engines.

Attachment at the boundary allows CEYO to observe defined inference events, capture policy-scoped context, normalize event structure, and seal artifacts without interfering with decision flow.

[02] Policy-Scoped Capture

Explicit Inclusion, Exclusion, Masking

Artifact generation begins with explicit policy definition. Operators define which fields are included, excluded, or masked; disclosure tiers; and policy version identifiers. Only fields within policy scope are captured.

By design, CEYO does not ingest model weights, training datasets, proprietary internals, unrelated telemetry, or full transcripts unless explicitly configured.

[03] Canonical Artifact Structure

Structured Records, Not Logs

Captured context is normalized into a canonical artifact schema designed for deterministic integrity verification. Artifacts include versioned metadata such as timestamp, policy identifier/version, disclosure tier, canonicalization version, integrity fields, and verification metadata.

Schema posture
Review-Ready Structure
Canonical artifacts are not free-form logs. They are structured records intended for review and independent integrity validation.
Key point Capture scope is embedded in the artifact so reviewers can verify not only integrity, but the declared capture boundary under which the artifact was produced.
[04] Deterministic Canonicalization

RFC 8785 JSON Canonicalization

Before hashing, JSON artifacts are canonicalized using RFC 8785. Canonicalization ensures that logically equivalent data produces identical byte representations across environments.

This step is critical for reproducible hashing and independent verification. Without deterministic canonicalization, verification becomes environment-dependent.

Canonicalization is treated as a structural requirement, not an optimization.
[05] Cryptographic Sealing

Hash + Signature

After canonicalization, artifacts are sealed using cryptographic hashing and digital signatures under a deployment-defined suite. Reference implementations may include SHA-256 for hashing and ECDSA (P-256) for signatures.

Hashing establishes tamper-evidence. Digital signatures establish origin authenticity. Cryptographic suites may evolve as standards evolve; integrity posture remains constant.

[06] Non-Custodial Key Architecture

Operator-Controlled Signing Authority

Signing keys are generated and retained within the client environment, typically within HSM, KMS, or TEE. CEYO does not possess, transmit, or store private signing keys.

This separation preserves clear liability boundaries between infrastructure provider and deploying institution. Artifact authenticity remains anchored to the operator.

[07] Disclosure Tier Enforcement

Verification Without Forced Exposure

Artifacts support tiered disclosure. Public or redacted tiers may expose summary metadata; controlled review tiers may expose expanded contextual fields aligned to authorized oversight requirements.

Disclosure boundaries are policy-defined before artifact generation. Verification does not require exposure of proprietary internals beyond policy scope.

[08] Integrity–Availability Balance

Fail-Open by Default

Integrity infrastructure must not compromise continuity by default. If artifact sealing fails, inference may continue uninterrupted; failures are logged; artifacts are marked unsealed; conditions remain reviewable.

Availability posture is an institutional decision. Operators may configure stricter enforcement where appropriate.

[09] Independent Verification Workflow

Deterministic Procedure

Verification is executable by independent parties using deterministic steps: canonicalize (RFC 8785), recompute hash, validate signature, and confirm policy identifier/version alignment.

Outcomes: Integrity Confirmed · Integrity Failed · Policy Scope Mismatch
Boundary: Verification confirms integrity and provenance under policy scope. It does not confirm semantic correctness or decision validity.
