[01] The Structural Deficit

The Structural Deficit

Modern AI systems generate logs, telemetry, and traces. These signals describe activity. They do not constitute evidence.

What telemetry does not reliably preserve is a canonicalized, integrity-bound, policy-scoped artifact suitable for independent verification at a later date. When decisions are disputed — in regulatory review, litigation, incident analysis, or institutional audit — partial logs and reconstructed traces are insufficient. Reconstruction from telemetry is inherently interpretive. It is not deterministic proof.

01 · Telemetry Gap
Activity is not evidence
Logs describe what happened. They are not integrity-sealed records designed for institutional verification. That distinction matters in post-hoc review, litigation, and audit contexts.
02 · Disclosure Tension
Verification without exposure
Institutions require validation without default disclosure of proprietary weights, confidential architecture, or trade-secret implementation. Telemetry-based systems do not resolve this structural tension.
03 · Reconstruction vs. Proof
Interpretive is not deterministic
Reconstructed traces are inherently interpretive. They are not deterministic. Oversight that relies on reconstruction relies on operator assertions, not verifiable record integrity.
04 · Liability Architecture
Architectural, not operational
Absent integrity-bound artifacts, disputes resolve around partial evidence and non-reproducible context. This is an architectural deficit, not an operational oversight correctable by better logging.
Without these properties, accountability relies on operator trust — not verifiable record integrity

[02] What CEYO Is

A Decoupled Evidentiary Substrate

CEYO is a decoupled evidentiary substrate. It attaches at the inference boundary — as a sidecar, wrapper, or gateway — and captures only what operator policy explicitly defines. It normalizes captured fields into a stable artifact schema, applies deterministic canonicalization per RFC 8785, and seals cryptographically under a non-custodial key architecture.

Verification confirms integrity and provenance under declared policy scope. It does not confirm correctness of model output.

01 · Canonical
Deterministic structure
Normalized schema. Stable byte representation independent of serialization order or deployment environment.
02 · Policy-scoped
Explicit capture boundary
Content defined by operator before capture. No implicit expansion. Policy version recorded in artifact.
03 · Tamper-evident
Cryptographic sealing
Hash and digital signature establish that artifacts have not been altered since the sealing event.
04 · Verifiable
No IP exposure required
Verification requires only public keys. Proprietary internals remain undisclosed by default.
[03] What CEYO Is Not

Records, Not Judgments

× A model evaluation system
× A fairness certification system
× A compliance automation tool
× A legal adjudication engine
× A regulatory certification authority
CEYO does not determine whether a decision was lawful, ethical, or fair. CEYO produces integrity-sealed artifacts that institutions may use as part of their oversight processes. Authorized institutions retain responsibility for all determinations.

The system is designed around one principle: evidence infrastructure must remain separate from decision authority. This separation preserves neutrality, ensures the evidentiary layer does not become an adjudicator of its own output, and avoids structural conflicts between record generation and policy enforcement.

CEYO provides the artifact. Institutions provide the oversight.

01 · Policy-scoped capture

Define before you collect

Operators define what fields are included, excluded, and masked before artifact generation begins. Capture policy is explicit and versioned. Artifacts reflect declared policy scope; they do not implicitly expand beyond it. The policy version is recorded within the artifact, making the capture boundary independently reviewable.

02 · Canonical structure

Deterministic normalization

Captured context is normalized into a deterministic JSON artifact schema. Canonicalization is applied in accordance with RFC 8785 (JSON Canonicalization Scheme), producing a stable byte representation independent of serialization order. This deterministic structure allows independent recomputation of integrity hashes without interpretive variance.

03 · Integrity sealing

Hash and signature

After canonicalization, hashing and digital signature are applied under a deployment-defined cryptographic suite. Hashing establishes tamper-evidence; digital signatures establish origin authenticity. Suite selection is configurable to align with institutional requirements. Integrity posture remains constant as standards evolve.

04 · Non-custodial keys

You control the signing

Signing keys are generated and retained within the client-controlled environment — HSM, KMS, or TEE. CEYO does not possess, transmit, or store private signing keys. This non-custodial architecture preserves separation between infrastructure provider and cryptographic authority.

05 · Constrained disclosure

Tiered verification access

Artifacts may be disclosed under public / redacted or controlled review tiers. Tiering allows verification of integrity without default exposure of proprietary weights, confidential architecture, or trade-secret internals. Verification confirms integrity and policy alignment; it does not expose proprietary content unless authorized.

06 · Fail-open architecture

Inference continuity first

Inference continuity remains independent of artifact sealing. If artifact generation fails, inference proceeds uninterrupted. Failure events are logged and reviewable; the artifact is marked as unsealed. Fail-open behavior preserves availability unless the operator explicitly configures a stricter posture.
