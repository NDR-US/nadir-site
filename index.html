[01] The Accountability Gap

The Accountability
Gap

Modern AI deployments optimize for throughput, latency, and availability. Accountability infrastructure is often secondary. When a decision is later questioned—through regulatory inquiry, internal audit, incident review, or litigation—organizations reconstruct events from partial logs and traces that were never designed to function as durable evidence.

Telemetry describes activity.
It does not preserve proof.
Reconstruction is interpretive, not deterministic.

Institutions require artifacts that are canonical in structure, explicit in capture scope, cryptographically sealed, and independently verifiable. Without those properties, oversight depends on operator assertions rather than verifiable integrity.

01 · Evidence Deficit
Activity ≠ Evidence
Logs describe what happened. They are not integrity-sealed records designed for institutional verification.
02 · Verification Constraint
Verify Without Exposure
Oversight often requires validation without disclosing proprietary architecture, weights, or trade-secret internals.
03 · Architectural Fix
Decoupled Evidentiary Layer
Evidence infrastructure should remain structurally independent from decision authority and model internals.

Records, Not Judgments
Verification confirms integrity and provenance under policy scope—not correctness, fairness, or compliance outcomes.

[02] What CEYO Is

A Decoupled
Verification Layer

CEYO attaches at the inference boundary—sidecar, gateway, or API wrapper—and generates deterministic artifacts representing defined inference-time decision-state context under explicit policy scope. It does not modify model weights. It does not intervene in decision logic.

Captured context is normalized into a canonical artifact schema, canonicalized deterministically, and sealed cryptographically for later verification. Verification confirms integrity and provenance under declared policy scope. It does not confirm correctness of output.

01 · Deterministic
Canonical Artifact Structure
Structured records designed for review—consistent across heterogeneous deployments.
02 · Policy-Bound
Explicit Capture Boundary
Only fields declared within policy scope are recorded; policy identifiers and versions are embedded.
03 · Integrity-Sealed
Hash + Signature
Tamper-evidence and authenticity using deployment-defined cryptographic suites.
04 · Constrained Disclosure
Tiered Access
Verification can occur without exposing proprietary internals beyond policy scope.
[03] What CEYO Is Not

Records,

01 · Policy-Scoped Capture
Define Before You Collect
Capture begins with explicit policy definition. Operators define included fields, excluded fields, masking rules, disclosure tier configuration, and policy version identifiers. Only policy-scoped fields are captured.
02 · Canonical Artifact Structure
Normalize Into Review-Ready Records
Captured context is normalized into a canonical artifact schema with structured metadata (timestamp, policy identifiers, environment fingerprint, disclosure tier, integrity fields). Artifacts are structured records intended for review—not free-form logs.
03 · Cryptographic Sealing
Hash and Signature
Artifacts are deterministically canonicalized before hashing and signing. Hashing establishes tamper-evidence. Digital signatures establish origin authenticity under a deployment-defined cryptographic suite.
04 · Non-Custodial Keys
Operator-Controlled Signing Authority
Signing keys are generated and retained within operator-controlled environments (HSM, KMS, or TEE). CEYO does not possess, transmit, or store private signing keys. Authenticity remains anchored to the deploying institution.
05 · Constrained Disclosure
Tiered Verification Access
Artifacts support tiered disclosure models. Public or redacted tiers may expose limited metadata. Controlled review tiers expose expanded contextual fields under authorized access. Disclosure boundaries are policy-defined before generation.
06 · Integrity–Availability Balance
Fail-Open by Default
Integrity infrastructure must not compromise operational continuity by default. If sealing fails, inference can continue uninterrupted; the failure is logged and the artifact is marked accordingly. Operators may configure stricter posture when required.
