[01] The Accountability Gap

The Accountability Gap

Modern AI deployments optimize for throughput, latency, and availability. Accountability infrastructure is frequently secondary.

When a decision is later questioned — through regulatory inquiry, internal audit, incident review, or litigation — organizations often reconstruct events from partial logs and distributed traces. Reconstruction is interpretive. It is not deterministic.

Institutions require more than reconstructed context. They require artifacts that are canonical in structure, explicit in capture scope, cryptographically sealed, and independently verifiable. Without those properties, oversight depends on operator assertions rather than verifiable integrity.

This is an architectural deficit, not an operational oversight.

[02] What CEYO Is

A Decoupled Evidentiary Substrate

CEYO attaches at the inference boundary — as a sidecar, wrapper, or gateway — and captures only what operator policy explicitly defines. It normalizes captured fields into a stable artifact schema, applies deterministic canonicalization, and seals cryptographically under a non-custodial key architecture.

Verification confirms integrity and provenance under declared policy scope. It does not confirm correctness of model output.

Not
Records are not judgments
CEYO does not determine whether a decision was lawful, ethical, or fair.
Principle
Structural independence
Evidence infrastructure must remain separate from decision authority.
[03] How It Works

Technical Properties

01 · Capture 02 · Structure 03 · Sealing 04 · Keys 05 · Disclosure 06 · Availability
01 · Policy-Scoped Capture
Define Before You Collect
Operators define what fields are included, excluded, and masked before artifact generation begins. Capture policy is explicit and versioned. Artifacts reflect declared policy scope; they do not implicitly expand beyond it. The policy version is recorded within the artifact, making the capture boundary independently reviewable.
02 · Canonical Structure
Deterministic Normalization
Captured telemetry is normalized into a deterministic JSON artifact schema. Canonicalization is applied in accordance with RFC 8785 (JSON Canonicalization Scheme), producing a stable byte representation independent of serialization order. This deterministic structure allows independent recomputation of integrity hashes without interpretive variance.
03 · Integrity Sealing
Hash and Signature
After canonicalization, SHA-256 hashing and ECDSA P-256 digital signature are applied as reference implementations. Suite selection is configurable based on regulatory and operational requirements. Hashing establishes tamper-evidence; digital signatures establish origin authenticity. The cryptographic suite may evolve as standards evolve. Integrity posture remains constant.
04 · Non-Custodial Keys
You Control the Signing
Signing keys are generated and retained within the client-controlled environment — HSM, KMS, or TEE. CEYO does not possess, transmit, or store private signing keys. This non-custodial architecture preserves a clear separation between infrastructure provider and cryptographic authority. Artifact authenticity remains anchored to the deploying institution.
05 · Constrained Disclosure
Tiered Verification Access
Artifacts may be disclosed under Public / Redacted or Controlled Review tiers. Disclosure tiering allows verification of integrity without default exposure of model weights, proprietary architecture, trade-secret implementation, or confidential system internals. Verification confirms artifact integrity and policy alignment; it does not expose proprietary content unless authorized.
06 · Fail-Open Architecture
Inference Continuity First
Inference continuity remains independent of artifact sealing. If artifact generation fails, inference proceeds uninterrupted. Failure events are logged and flagged; the artifact is marked as unsealed and the condition becomes reviewable. Fail-open behavior preserves availability unless the operator explicitly configures a stricter posture.
