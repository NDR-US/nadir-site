Telemetry

AI systems increasingly influence decisions in regulated, safety-critical, and institutional environments. When those decisions are reviewed, the underlying infrastructure often produces telemetry, logs, and traces that were never designed to function as durable evidence. CEYO exists to address that structural gap — a weight-neutral, policy-scoped integrity layer that attaches beside inference systems and produces canonical artifacts representing defined inference-time decision-state context, sealed for institutional verification under constrained disclosure.

[The Accountability Gap]

Telemetry Describes Activity. It Does Not Preserve Proof.

Modern AI deployments optimize for throughput, latency, and availability. Accountability infrastructure is frequently secondary.

When a decision is later questioned — through regulatory inquiry, internal audit, incident review, or litigation — organizations often reconstruct events from partial logs and distributed traces. Reconstruction is interpretive. It is not deterministic.

Institutions require more than reconstructed context They require artifacts that are canonical, scoped, sealed, and independently verifiable Without those properties, oversight depends on operator assertions rather than verifiable integrity
Canonical in structure
Review requires stable, deterministic structure — not free-form logs that vary by environment, serialization, or instrumentation.
Explicit in capture scope
Scope must be defined before generation: included fields, excluded fields, masked fields, tier configuration, and policy version identifiers.
Cryptographically sealed
Tamper-evidence and provenance require hashing and signatures on canonical bytes — not post-hoc narrative about what happened.
Independently verifiable
Oversight must be executable by third parties under deterministic procedure, without default disclosure of proprietary internals.
This is an architectural deficit, not an operational oversight.

[A Decoupled Verification Layer]

Evidence Infrastructure Beside Inference

CEYO introduces an evidentiary substrate that operates beside inference engines. It does not modify model weights. It does not intervene in decision logic. It does not replace governance frameworks.

It captures only what policy defines, normalizes that context into a stable artifact schema, applies deterministic canonicalization, and seals the artifact for tamper-evidence.

Verification confirms integrity and provenance under declared policy scope. It does not confirm correctness of output.

Policy-scoped capture
Operator-defined inclusion, exclusion, masking, disclosure tiers, and policy version identifiers.
Canonicalization
Deterministic JSON canonicalization (RFC 8785) for reproducible byte output across environments.
Sealing
Hashing + digital signatures to establish tamper-evidence and origin authenticity.
Neutrality
Evidence remains structurally independent from decision authority and adjudication.

[Decoupled Verification Architecture]

Deterministic Artifacts. Independent Verification.

05 · Availability
01 · Attachment at the Inference Boundary
Sidecar · Gateway · API Wrapper
CEYO integrates at the inference boundary so evidence infrastructure does not require mutation of model weights, training pipelines, or core inference engines. The inference engine remains unchanged. The integrity layer operates externally.
02 · Policy-Scoped Capture
Define Before You Collect
Artifact generation begins with explicit policy definition: which fields are included, excluded, masked; disclosure tier configuration; and policy version identifiers. Capture scope is versioned and embedded inside the artifact for reviewable boundaries.
03 · Deterministic Canonicalization
RFC 8785 JSON Canonicalization
Before hashing, artifacts are canonicalized using RFC 8785 (JSON Canonicalization Scheme), ensuring logically equivalent structures produce identical byte output across environments. Verification remains reproducible and not environment-dependent.
04 · Cryptographic Sealing
Hashing + Signatures
After canonicalization, artifacts are sealed via cryptographic hashing and digital signatures. Reference implementations include SHA-256 and ECDSA (P-256). Signing keys remain non-custodial within operator-controlled HSM/KMS/TEE environments.
05 · Integrity–Availability Balance
Fail-Open by Default
Integrity infrastructure must not halt inference by default. If sealing fails, inference may continue uninterrupted while seal failures are logged, flagged, and reviewable. Availability posture remains an institutional decision.
