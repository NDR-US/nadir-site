[01] The Structural Deficit

The Structural
Deficit

Modern AI systems generate logs, telemetry, and traces. These signals describe activity. They do not constitute evidence.

Telemetry may show that a model executed It may show input and output It may show timestamps and service states

What telemetry does not reliably preserve is a canonicalized, integrity-bound, policy-scoped artifact suitable for independent verification at a later date. When decisions are disputed — in regulatory review, litigation, incident analysis, or institutional audit — partial logs and reconstructed traces are insufficient. Reconstruction from telemetry is inherently interpretive. It is not deterministic proof. This is the structural deficit.

01 · Telemetry Gap
Activity ≠ Evidence
Logs describe what happened. They are not integrity-sealed records designed for institutional verification. That distinction matters in post-hoc review, litigation, and audit contexts.
02 · Disclosure Tension
Verification Without Exposure
Institutions require verification without exposure of proprietary weights, confidential architecture, or trade-secret implementation. Telemetry-based systems do not resolve this structural tension.
03 · Reconstruction vs. Proof
Interpretive ≠ Deterministic
Reconstructed traces are inherently interpretive. They are not deterministic. Oversight that relies on reconstruction relies on operator assertions, not verifiable record integrity.
04 · Liability Architecture
Architectural, Not Operational
Absent integrity-bound artifacts, disputes resolve around partial evidence and non-reproducible context. This is an architectural deficit, not an operational oversight correctable by better logging.
Without these properties, accountability relies on operator trust — not verifiable record integrity
  • Deterministic structure
    Consistent schema producible by any conforming implementation
  • Integrity sealing
    Cryptographic hash and signature establishing tamper-evidence and origin
  • Explicit disclosure scope
    Versioned policy capturing what is and is not included
  • Separation of internals and oversight
    Verification without requiring exposure of proprietary IP
[02] What CEYO Is

A Decoupled
Evidentiary Substrate

CEYO is a decoupled evidentiary substrate. It attaches at the inference boundary — as a sidecar, wrapper, or gateway — and captures only what operator policy explicitly defines. It normalizes captured fields into a stable artifact schema, applies deterministic canonicalization per RFC 8785, and seals cryptographically under a non-custodial key architecture.

Verification confirms integrity and provenance under declared policy scope. It does not confirm correctness of model output.

01 · Canonical
Deterministic Structure
Normalized schema. Stable byte representation independent of serialization order or deployment environment.
02 · Policy-Scoped
Explicit Capture Boundary
Content defined by operator before capture. No implicit expansion. Policy version recorded in artifact.
03 · Tamper-Evident
Cryptographic Sealing
Hash and digital signature establish that artifact has not been altered since the sealing event.
04 · Independently Verifiable
No IP Exposure Required
Verification workflows require only public keys. Proprietary internals remain undisclosed by default.
[03] What CEYO Is Not

Records,
Not Judgments

× A model evaluation system
× A fairness certification system
× A compliance automation tool
× A legal adjudication engine
× A regulatory certification authority

CEYO does not determine whether a decision was lawful, ethical, or fair. CEYO produces integrity-sealed artifacts that institutions may use as part of their oversight processes. Authorized institutions retain responsibility for all determinations.

The system is designed around one principle: evidence infrastructure must remain separate from decision authority. This separation preserves neutrality, ensures the evidentiary layer does not become an adjudicator of its own output, and avoids structural conflicts between record generation and policy enforcement.

CEYO provides the artifact. Institutions provide the oversight.

01 · Policy-Scoped Capture
Define Before You Collect
Operators define what fields are included, excluded, and masked before artifact generation begins. Capture policy is explicit and versioned. Artifacts reflect declared policy scope; they do not implicitly expand beyond it. The policy version is recorded within the artifact, making the capture boundary independently reviewable.
02 · Canonical Structure
Deterministic Normalization
Captured telemetry is normalized into a deterministic JSON artifact schema. Canonicalization is applied in accordance with RFC 8785 (JSON Canonicalization Scheme), producing a stable byte representation independent of serialization order. This deterministic structure allows independent recomputation of integrity hashes without interpretive variance.
03 · Integrity Sealing
Hash and Signature
After canonicalization, SHA-256 hashing and ECDSA P-256 digital signature are applied as reference implementations. Suite selection is configurable based on regulatory and operational requirements. Hashing establishes tamper-evidence; digital signatures establish origin authenticity. The cryptographic suite may evolve as standards evolve. Integrity posture remains constant.
04 · Non-Custodial Keys
You Control the Signing
Signing keys are generated and retained within the client-controlled environment — HSM, KMS, or TEE. CEYO does not possess, transmit, or store private signing keys. This non-custodial architecture preserves a clear separation between infrastructure provider and cryptographic authority. Artifact authenticity remains anchored to the deploying institution.
05 · Constrained Disclosure
Tiered Verification Access
Artifacts may be disclosed under Public / Redacted or Controlled Review tiers. Disclosure tiering allows verification of integrity without default exposure of model weights, proprietary architecture, trade-secret implementation, or confidential system internals. Verification confirms artifact integrity and policy alignment; it does not expose proprietary content unless authorized.
06 · Fail-Open Architecture
Inference Continuity First
Inference continuity remains independent of artifact sealing. If artifact generation fails, inference proceeds uninterrupted. Failure events are logged and flagged; the artifact is marked as unsealed and the condition becomes reviewable. Fail-open behavior preserves availability unless the operator explicitly configures a stricter posture.
