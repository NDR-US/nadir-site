[01] THE ACCOUNTABILITY GAP

The Accountability Gap

Modern AI deployments optimize for throughput, latency, and availability. Accountability infrastructure is frequently secondary.

When a decision is later questioned — through regulatory inquiry, internal audit, incident review, or litigation — organizations often reconstruct events from partial logs and distributed traces. Reconstruction is interpretive. It is not deterministic proof.

Institutions require artifacts that are canonical in structure, explicit in capture scope, cryptographically sealed, and independently verifiable.

Without those properties, oversight depends on operator assertions rather than verifiable integrity. This is an architectural deficit, not an operational oversight.

Telemetry describes activity.

It does not preserve proof.

This is an architectural deficit — not an operational oversight correctable by “better logging.”

[02] SYSTEM ARCHITECTURE

A Decoupled Evidentiary Substrate

CEYO attaches at the inference boundary — as a sidecar, wrapper, or gateway — and captures only what operator policy explicitly defines. It normalizes captured fields into a stable artifact schema, applies deterministic canonicalization, and seals cryptographically under a non-custodial key architecture.

Verification confirms integrity and provenance under declared policy scope. It does not confirm correctness of model output.

[03] HOW IT WORKS

Technical Properties

01 · Capture 02 · Structure 03 · Sealing 04 · Keys 05 · Disclosure 06 · Availability
01 · POLICY-SCOPED CAPTURE

Define Before You Collect

Operators define what fields are included, excluded, and masked before artifact generation begins. Capture policy is explicit and versioned. Artifacts reflect declared policy scope; they do not implicitly expand beyond it. The policy version is recorded within the artifact, making the capture boundary independently reviewable.

02 · CANONICAL STRUCTURE

Deterministic Normalization

Captured context is normalized into a deterministic JSON artifact schema. Canonicalization may be applied in accordance with RFC 8785 (JSON Canonicalization Scheme), producing a stable byte representation independent of serialization order. This deterministic structure enables independent recomputation of integrity hashes without interpretive variance.

03 · INTEGRITY SEALING

Hash and Signature

After canonicalization, hashing and digital signature are applied as reference implementations. Suite selection is configurable based on regulatory and operational requirements. Hashing establishes tamper-evidence; digital signatures establish origin authenticity.

04 · NON-CUSTODIAL KEYS

You Control the Signing

Signing keys are generated and retained within the client-controlled environment — HSM, KMS, or TEE. CEYO does not possess, transmit, or store private signing keys. This preserves a clear separation between infrastructure provider and cryptographic authority.

05 · CONSTRAINED DISCLOSURE

Tiered Verification Access

Artifacts may be disclosed under Public / Redacted or Controlled Review tiers. Disclosure tiering allows verification of integrity without default exposure of model weights, proprietary architecture, or confidential system internals. Disclosure boundaries are defined pre-generation.

06 · FAIL-OPEN ARCHITECTURE

Inference Continuity First

Inference continuity remains independent of artifact sealing. If artifact generation fails, inference proceeds uninterrupted. Failure events are logged and flagged; the artifact is marked as unsealed and the condition becomes reviewable.

Controlled Review

Detailed documentation, verification contracts, capture schema specifications, and benchmark methodology are available under controlled disclosure. Access is granted following review request evaluation.
