Why NADIR exists

A procurement reviewer is not only assessing technology. They are assessing institutional risk, evidentiary defensibility, and whether the vendor understands liability boundaries.

I built NADIR around a simple principle: infrastructure should make oversight possible without becoming the judge. NADIR is not a “safety layer” and it does not certify compliance. It is an evidentiary substrate—designed to preserve a decision-state record that can be verified later by authorized institutions.

The technical details matter because evidence fails at the edges. That is why NADIR treats canonicalization as a first-class requirement. JSON artifacts are canonicalized deterministically (RFC 8785) before hashing, so trivial differences do not break integrity claims across heterogeneous environments.

NADIR is designed to minimize liability surfaces. Signing keys are intended to remain non-custodial: generated and retained within the operator’s controlled environment (HSM/KMS where available). That separation is deliberate. If NADIR does not hold keys, NADIR cannot forge records and is harder to coerce into altering evidence.

If NADIR succeeds, oversight becomes more precise, disputes become less ambiguous, and institutions can verify integrity without default exposure of proprietary systems. That is the purpose. The record is the foundation. The judgment remains human.
