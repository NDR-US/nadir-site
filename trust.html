Trust Center

Procurement reviewers evaluate whether a system’s trust claims are bounded, auditable, and operationally separated from client risk. This page summarizes NADIR’s trust posture, disclosure controls, and reporting pathways. It is a public summary only; technical specifics are available under controlled review.

[SECURITY]

Security posture and separation

NADIR’s public posture focuses on verifiable mechanics, controlled disclosure, and operational separation. Security controls vary by deployment model, environment, and client requirements. Where clients require formal assurance, supporting evidence is provided under controlled review.

[ISOLATION]

Sidecar separation

NADIR is designed to attach at the inference boundary (sidecar, gateway, or wrapper) so artifact generation remains decoupled from model weights and core inference logic.

[RESOURCE QUOTAS]

Back-pressure posture

Deployments can enforce resource isolation (e.g., cgroup-based limits) so the artifact layer cannot consume CPU or memory reserved for core inference, reducing operational back-pressure risk.

[FAIL-OPEN]

Integrity–availability balance

Default posture is fail-open: if sealing fails, inference continues, the event is logged, and the record is flagged as unsealed for review. Alternate policies can be specified under controlled requirements.

[BOUNDARY]
  • NADIR does not certify security, compliance, or legal sufficiency on behalf of deployers.
  • Security controls and attestations depend on deployment architecture, environment, and client governance requirements.
  • Oversight conclusions are made by authorized institutions and operators.
[DISCLOSURE]

Constrained disclosure and data residency posture

Trust in evidentiary infrastructure requires explicit boundaries: what is collected, what is excluded, who can see what, and where data resides. NADIR’s public posture is to support policy-scoped capture with tiered disclosure and deployment-defined residency.

[MINIMIZATION]

Policy-scoped capture

Capture scope is defined by configuration: included fields, excluded fields, masking rules, disclosure tier, and policy version identifier. Unnecessary collection is explicitly excluded.

[TIERS]

Tiered visibility

Public views can remain redacted. Authorized reviewers can receive expanded fields required for verification under controlled review, preserving trade-secret boundaries.

[RESIDENCY]

Client-governed storage

Data residency and retention posture are deployment-defined. Where required, artifacts can be retained within client-controlled environments and governed by client policy.

[KEY CUSTODY DECLARATION]

NADIR is designed for non-custodial deployments: signing keys are generated and retained within the client’s controlled environment (HSM/KMS or equivalent). NADIR does not require possession of client private keys. Key custody policies, rotation requirements, and verification trust anchors are defined by deployment configuration and governance.

[INTEGRITY]

Integrity verification and cryptographic agility

Evidence posture depends on determinism and verifiability. NADIR’s public language remains bounded: it describes the mechanics used to support integrity and provenance checks, while algorithm suites and attestations are specified in controlled technical documentation.

[CANONICALIZATION]

Deterministic serialization

Verification contracts can specify deterministic canonicalization for JSON artifacts (e.g., RFC 8785) prior to hashing so integrity checks are stable across systems and implementations.

[SEALING]

Hash and signature posture

Artifacts can be sealed using cryptographic hashes and digital signatures to support tamper detection and provenance validation. Exact suites are deployment-defined and may vary by regulatory requirements.

[PQC]

Post-quantum readiness posture

NADIR’s posture is to support cryptographic agility and a roadmap toward post-quantum readiness where required by procurement profiles or future standards. Specific choices are documented under controlled review.

[BOUNDARY]
  • NADIR confirms artifact integrity and provenance under defined policy; it does not confirm correctness, legality, or semantic equivalence of outputs.
  • Regulatory sufficiency and evidentiary weight are determined by relevant authorities and deploying organizations.
